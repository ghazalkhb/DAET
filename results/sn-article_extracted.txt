                                   Abstract
Performance debugging in production systems is hampered by high tracing over-
head and the challenge of determining when to collect data. This paper presents
an extended evaluation of the dynamic adaptive execution tracing (DAET)
framework first introduced in our conference paper [1]. The framework oper-
ates in three phases: (1) Tracing-Function Selection via call-stack profiling and
coefficient-of-variation (CoV) ranking, (2) Change Detection based on anomaly
scoring with a dynamic threshold, and (3) Trace Configuration Adjustment that
activates tracepoints only when the anomaly score exceeds a tolerance-based gate.
This journal extension contributes five new components beyond the conference
work: (i) a dual-dataset fair comparison of five change-detection engines—
ARIMA(1,1,1), last-value (LAST), simple moving average (SMA), exponentially
weighted moving average (EWMA), and a compact Transformer encoder—on two
Mozilla performance-telemetry benchmarks: Firefox-Android (342 signatures)
and mozilla-beta (1,477 signatures), using an identical 60%/20%/20% chrono-
logical split with independent per-dataset held-out validation-set tuning; (ii) a
statistical significance analysis using 2,000-resample bootstrap confidence inter-
vals and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests on the main Firefox-Android evaluation;
(iii) a trace-on replay experiment for the four statistical detectors (ARIMA,
LAST, SMA, EWMA) quantifying storage reduction, alert coverage, and trigger
frequency as supplementary deployment evidence; (iv) a three-way chronological

     ing; and (v) a fair multi-seed evaluation of a compact Transformer encoder (five
     independent seeds) under the identical 60%/20%/20% protocol as all classical
     detectors. On Firefox-Android (136 of 342 test-alerted signatures), SMA(w =
     40) achieves the highest F1 = 0.104 and detection rate 72.8% among classi-
     cal methods; EWMA(α = 0.05) is comparable (F1 = 0.103); ARIMA achieves
     F1 = 0.089; LAST is weakest (F1 = 0.072). The compact Transformer (WIN-

     ARIMA F1 = 0.076; LAST F1 = 0.069. The compact Transformer (WIN-

     the Firefox-Android main evaluation confirms ARIMA and SMA are statistically
     indistinguishable in F1 (p = 0.547), and both significantly outperform LAST
     and EWMA. All detectors reduce trace storage by 67–90%; the compact Trans-
     former achieves 67–71% vs. 88–90% for classical methods. The ablation study
     shows that the default parameters (β = 0.30, θ = 0.30, ARIMA order (1,1,1))
     represent an operationally justified high-recall configuration; alternative settings
     (e.g. β = 0.20, θ ≥ 0.40) yield materially higher F1 but lower recall.

1 Introduction
Modern software systems rely on continuous performance monitoring to detect
regressions before they impact end users. Execution tracing—recording timestamped
function invocations, system calls, and hardware events—provides the most detailed
diagnostic data for root-cause analysis [2–4]. However, always-on tracing imposes non-
trivial runtime overhead: the LTTng kernel tracer requires as little as 158 ns per
tracepoint materialization [5], yet at high event rates the cumulative I/O and storage
cost quickly becomes prohibitive [6]. The fundamental dilemma is that tracing must
be enabled before a regression manifests to capture causally relevant data, yet leaving
it enabled at all times wastes resources and generates enormous volumes of irrelevant
data.

1.1 Conference Paper Foundation
In our prior work [1] we introduced the Dynamic Adaptive Execution Tracing (DAET)
framework, which addresses this dilemma by automating the decision of which func-
tions to monitor and when to activate their tracepoints. The framework consists
of three phases: a profiling phase that builds a ranked candidate list of functions


                                               2
via call-stack sampling and CoV-based prioritisation; a change-detection phase that
feeds function-execution time-series data into a pluggable change-detection engine
(ARIMA(1,1,1) in the original work) and maintains a decaying anomaly score; and
a trace-configuration phase that toggles tracepoints when the anomaly score crosses
a predefined threshold. Four Firefox/Bugzilla case studies demonstrated that the
framework (a) correctly ranks known culprit functions in the top-5% monitored set,
(b) triggers tracing only during anomalous intervals (44% storage reduction on a
representative tracepoint), and (c) handles both application-level and kernel-level com-
ponents through a multi-level mechanism. Those case studies were illustrative rather
than prospective: a known bug report was used post hoc to confirm that the frame-
work would have surfaced the culprit. Rigorous, prospective, large-scale evaluation
was explicitly deferred as future work [1].

1.2 Journal Extension: Motivation and Scope
Three open questions motivate this journal extension.
    Engine choice. ARIMA was selected as the change-detection engine in the
conference paper because it predicts based on past trends and does not adapt to
new anomalies, preserving sensitivity to genuine regime shifts. However, simpler
alternatives—last observed value, simple moving average, EWMA—share similar prop-
erties and are far cheaper to compute. It is unknown whether the added complexity
of ARIMA provides measurably superior detection quality on real-world telemetry at
scale.
    Deployment trade-offs. Even when a detector correctly identifies a performance
regression, the practical benefit depends on whether the trace recorder was actually
active at that moment. No prior study has systematically quantified the storage reduc-
tion versus alert-coverage trade-off that each detector implies under a unified replay
protocol.
    Parameter robustness. The anomaly score decay factor β , detection threshold
θ, and tolerance window τ were chosen empirically in the conference paper. Formal
ablation evidence characterising the stable operating region and the sensitivity of
results to parameter variation across a large dataset was absent.

1.3 Research Questions
This journal paper addresses the following extension research questions (denoted RQ5–
RQ8 to continue the numbering from [1]):

RQ6 Are the performance differences between detectors statistically significant at the

1.4 Journal Contributions
The primary contribution of this journal extension over [1] is an empirical com-
parative extension : a large-scale validation of the DAET framework’s detection
phase, a comparative detector study, a formal statistical analysis, and a replay-based
storage/coverage analysis. The core DAET method—the three-phase framework archi-
tecture, CoV-based function selection, and anomaly-score gating—was introduced
in [1]; this paper does not claim methodological novelty in the detection mechanism.
    Specifically, the extension contributes:
1. A reproducible large-scale comparative validation of four classical change
   detectors—ARIMA(1,1,1), LAST, SMA, EWMA—on 214 Mozilla Firefox-Android
   Treeherder telemetry signatures (214 time-series × 4 methods = 856 evaluated
   detector–signature pairs).
2. A formal statistical analysis: 2,000-resample bootstrap 95% confidence intervals
   for precision, recall, F1, and false-alarm rate; Wilcoxon signed-rank tests for all
   six pairwise comparisons among the four statistical detectors—confirming that
   ARIMA vs. SMA differences in F1 and precision are not statistically significant
   (p > 0.05).
3. An ablation study sweeping β ∈ {0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7}, θ ∈ {0.10, . . . , 0.50},
   τ ∈ {1, 2, 3, 5, 7, 10}, five ARIMA orders, and six SMA windows—characterising
   the stable operating regions of the parameter space and the operational tradeoffs
   involved in parameter selection.
4. A trace-on replay experiment on the full test-set chronology for the four statistical
   detectors, measuring storage reduction (89–91%), trigger frequency (≈5.7–6.1%),
   and alert coverage (70–93%), plus an efficiency-frontier analysis characterising the
   storage–coverage Pareto trade-off.
5. A three-way chronological split validation (60%/20%/20%) in which SMA w and
   EWMA α are independently tuned on a held-out validation set and metrics are
   reported on a clean held-out test set. This directly addresses the internal valid-
   ity limitation of exploratory parameter selection—and reveals that properly tuned
   EWMA(α = 0.05) achieves F1 comparable to SMA(w = 40) on Firefox-Android,
   and likewise on mozilla-beta, partially revising the original cross-detector ranking.
6. A fair multi-seed (five seeds) dual-dataset three-way evaluation of a compact Trans-
   former encoder under the identical 60%/20%/20% chronological split protocol as all
   classical methods, closing the fairness gap of the prior single-seed 70%/30% Trans-
   former evaluation. The compact Transformer uses the same Algorithm-3 anomaly-
   score gating as the classical detectors, with architecture and hyperparameters tuned
   per dataset on the held-out validation set. On Firefox-Android (WINDOW=20,
   dmodel = 16) the compact Transformer achieves F1 = 0.105 (±0.002) and detection

   The qualitative ranking SMA ≈ EWMA ≥ ARIMA > LAST is consistent across

2 Related Works
2.1 Software Analysis
Previous work on software analysis includes static and dynamic analysis approaches.
Static analysis tools such as Facebook Infer [7] and FindBugs [8] identify performance
issues by detecting anti-patterns in source code, such as inefficient loops or wasteful call
sequences. While they impose no runtime impact, they often generate false positives
due to lack of runtime context [9–11].
    Mace et al. [12] proposed pivot tracing, which instruments systems without kernel
modifications using an SQL-like query language. However, it requires prior knowledge
of the program’s source code. Mirgorodskiy et al. [13] introduced self-propelled instru-
mentation, where an autonomous agent propagates through code via execution flow;
this fails when all execution flows halt simultaneously [9].
    Dynamic runtime analysis [6, 14] monitors program behaviour to pinpoint root
causes. DTrace [2] dynamically modifies the OS kernel to place probes only when
needed. LTTng [3] and Ftrace [4] provide low-overhead kernel-level tracing. While
dynamic approaches can address issues in production environments, they introduce
runtime overhead [6, 14].

2.2 Clustering, Anomaly, and Trend Detection
Analysing system-level metrics provides insights for predicting faults and detecting
performance regressions. Nguyen et al. [15] used low-level metrics to detect abnormal
change propagation paths. Zhang et al. [16] applied K-Means density clustering to
detect anomalies in performance data. Tate et al. [17] employed a cohort intelligence
model fed with kernel trace data.
    Ehlers et al. [18] used ARIMA to detect trends in response times and adaptively
monitor system performance—an approach that inspires our own anomaly-score func-
tion. Their work focuses on application-level tracing only; our framework extends to
multi-level (application and kernel) adaptive tracing.
    Ates et al. [19] proposed Pythia, a real-time workflow-centric tracing framework
that continuously gathers critical-path skeletons and groups them by performance
expectation. Pythia is limited to application-level tracing and assumes no problematic
groups in the first run, whereas DAET is fully online and does not require prior
labelling.



                                             5
2.3 Time-Series Anomaly Detection in Software Systems
Anomaly detection in time-series metrics from production systems has attracted con-
siderable attention. Liu et al. [20] introduced Opprentice, a supervised offline learning
approach for selecting operator-preferred anomaly detectors from a library of can-
didates applied to performance metrics. Lavin and Ahmad [21] proposed NAB, a
benchmark for evaluating real-time anomaly detectors on streaming data, providing
a standardised scoring framework that accounts for detection latency. KDD datasets
and the Yahoo EGADS system [22] demonstrate the breadth of statistical and ML-
based approaches applied to large-scale time-series anomaly detection in production.
Cid-Fuentes et al. [23] proposed adaptive SVM-based anomaly detection in distributed
systems—the closest prior work to our setting, though their approach requires labelled
training data that is unavailable in our Mozilla deployment scenario. DAET’s change-
detection phase differs in being fully unsupervised and online, requiring no labelled
anomalies, no manual threshold selection beyond the ablation-justified defaults, and
no periodic retraining.

2.4 Metrics Selection
Selecting the right metrics is critical for informed adaptive tracing decisions.
Laguna et al. [24] used PCA on abnormal/normal traces to reduce dimensionality; how-
ever, their approach requires significant manual effort. Munawar et al. [25] evaluated
random, correlation-based, hierarchical, and MST-based selection methods, finding
MST-based pairwise correlation selection superior—inspiring our own correlation-
based filtering. Dymshits et al. [26] observed that system call counts become stable
unless perturbed by external factors, supporting our assumption of metric stability in
healthy systems. Han et al. [27] proposed StackMine, which mines call-stack snapshots
to estimate function execution times—an idea we adapt in Phase 1 of our framework.

3 Adaptive Execution Tracing Framework
This section describes the three-phase DAET framework. Figure 1 shows the overall
architecture.

3.1 Phase 1: Tracing-Function Selection
3.1.1 Call-Stack Monitoring
A suitable snapshot period is determined by the application’s characteristics (e.g., 10
ms for a browser). The application is executed under representative workloads, and
all observed function and system calls are recorded.

3.1.2 Family-Group Identification and Sorting
A family group captures the parent–child call hierarchy. For a snapshot SP =
{Call-Stack i | 1 ≤ i ≤ n}, the family group of Call-Stack 1 = ⟨F a , F b , F c ⟩ is
{(F a , F b ), (F b , F c )}. Groups are sorted by invocation frequency, prioritising functions
with the highest potential performance impact. Algorithm 1 formalises this procedure.

3.1.3 CoV Ranking
Functions are further ranked by their Coefficient of Variation (CoV) of execution time
CoVM = σM /µM , ensuring the most volatile functions are monitored (Algorithm 2).

3.1.4 List Cutoff
A cutoff (e.g., top-5% of all monitored functions) ensures that only the most
performance-critical candidates are tracked, limiting monitoring overhead without
sacrificing coverage of high-impact calls.

3.2 Phase 2: Change Detection
3.2.1 ARIMA(1,1,1) Model
For each monitored function, an ARIMA(1,1,1) model predicts the next execution-time
sample based on historical data:

                      x̂t+1 = xt + φ ∆xt + θ εt ,       εt = xt − x̂t .              (1)

First-order differencing captures linear trends; the model flags deviations from the
expected trend without adapting to them, preserving sensitivity to genuine regime
shifts. A dynamic threshold adaptively scales with recent variability:
                                                           
                          Θt = max Θmin , kσ · std(δt−w:t ) ,                        (2)

where δt = |xt − x̂t |, w = 30 (lookback window), and kσ = 3.0. A reading is flagged
anomalous when δt > Θt .
    Why ARIMA? ARIMA predicts based on past trends and—unlike machine-
learning approaches—does not adapt to new changes, which preserves detection
sensitivity. ARIMA identifies all trend changes regardless of cause (load shift, anomaly,
attack), signalling that additional tracing data are needed. We select ARIMA(1,1,1)
rather than higher-order variants so that gradual, organic workload growth is accom-
modated (the integrated component re-centres predictions) while quadratic anomalies
remain detectable.

3.2.2 Anomaly Score
The anomaly score is an exponentially weighted accumulator that rises when anomalies
occur frequently and decays otherwise:

                          st = β · 1[δt > Θt ] + (1 − β ) · st−1 ,                   (3)

where β ∈ (0, 1) controls the reaction rate. Tracing is activated when st ≥ θ and
deactivated when the score falls below θ. This gating prevents over-reaction to single
spikes while ensuring persistent anomalies are always captured (see Figure 2).

Algorithm 3 Online Change Detection and Trace Gating
 1: Initialise st ← 0 for all monitored functions
 2: for all incoming sample xt per function do
 3:     x̂t ← ARIMA(xt−1 , xt−2 , . . .)
 4:     δt ← |xt − x̂t |
 5:     Θt ← max(Θmin , kσ · std(δt−w:t ))
 6:     st ← β · 1[δt > Θt ] + (1 − β ) · st−1
 7:     if st ≥ θ then
 8:         Enable tracepoint(s) for this function’s family-group
 9:     else
10:         Disable tracepoint(s)


3.3 Phase 3: Trace Configuration Change
When the change-detection phase identifies a trend deviation, tracing is activated for
the associated function family-groups up to a pre-set depth limit. At the system level,
detected system-call anomalies are mapped to the corresponding kernel component via
the Linux system-call table [28], enabling targeted kernel-level tracing. Algorithm 3
combines all three decisions into a single online loop.

5 Large-Scale Comparative Evaluation
5.1 Dataset
We use two publicly available Mozilla Treeherder performance-telemetry datasets [29].
Treeherder is Mozilla’s continuous-integration platform; it records performance signa-
tures for each test suite across every Firefox build and raises regression alerts when a
Bayesian change-point detector flags a statistically significant performance change.
    The raw Firefox-Android dataset contains 17,989 total alert records across 342
unique telemetry signatures. For the main comparative evaluation (Sections 5.2–
5.5), signatures were retained if they satisfy two inclusion criteria: (1) at least one
regression-alert event is associated with the signature, and (2) the time-series contains
at least 50 data points (sufficient for a meaningful 70%/30% train/test split with at
least 15 test samples). After filtering, we obtain 214 matched signatures with a
total of 7,612 regression-alert records.
    For the three-way chronological split evaluation (Section 5.6), we relax the
length threshold to 30 data points and extend the evaluation to a second repository.
This yields 342 Firefox-Android and 1,477 mozilla-beta matched signatures under a
60%/20%/20% train/val/test split. Table 2 summarises both dataset configurations.

5.3 Evaluation Protocol
For each of the 214 signatures and each of the 4 statistical detectors, we: (1) fit the
model on the first 70% of the time series; (2) run the detector on the remaining 30%
(test set) in a single pass; (3) align the binary detect/no-detect output with ground-
truth alert timestamps using a tolerance window of τ = 5 samples either side (an
alert within ±5 samples of a detection is counted as a true positive); (4) compute
precision, recall, F1, false-alarm rate (FAR), and storage reduction. All metrics are
macro-averaged : computed per signature and then aggregated to a mean across the
214 signatures.
    The detection delay for a matched alert at time ta is defined as d = s − ta , where
s is the start time-step of the earliest detected interval that covers [ta − τ, ta + τ ]; a
negative delay indicates pre-detection (the detector fires before the centre of the alert
window). The false-alarm rate (FAR) is an interval-level rate:

 Storage Reduction: 1 − (ON samples)/(total test samples). This is estimated

  suppressed, but does not account for variable trace event size, I/O batching, or
  compression. Actual byte savings in deployment will depend on the storage backend.
 Trigger Frequency: rate of 0 → 1 transitions per test sample (measures how often

 Alert Coverage: for each alerted signature (one with ≥ 1 alert in the test period),
  the fraction of its ground-truth test-set alerts that were covered by at least one trace-

8 Discussion
8.1 Operational Engine Selection
The experimental results support the following guidance for practitioners deploying
the DAET framework. Among the four statistical methods evaluated under the rig-
orous 60%/20%/20% protocol, SMA leads on recall, detection rate, and replay alert
coverage; EWMA matches SMA in F1 when properly tuned; ARIMA is third; LAST
is weakest. Three deployment profiles emerge depending on accuracy objectives and
resource constraints.
    Recommended default (most deployments): use SMA(w=20). It achieves
detection rate 96%, mean recall 0.657, F1 0.186, and mean per-signature alert recall

                                         26
8.2 ARIMA vs. SMA: Why Not Simply Use SMA?
A natural question arises: if SMA and ARIMA are not significantly different in F1
and precision (Wilcoxon p > 0.5), and SMA leads on recall and detection rate, why
retain ARIMA as the primary engine?
    We identify three engineering-motivated reasons: (1) SMA’s moving average gener-
ates a prediction only after the window is fully populated and reacts symmetrically to
both up and down deviations. ARIMA(1,1,1)’s integrated first-order model can cap-
ture monotonic trends (e.g., gradual CPU drift) and will continue predicting along
the new trend after a level shift, thereby reducing spurious alarms for organic load
growth. (2) In settings where non-stationarity is pronounced (servers with daily sea-
sonality, progressive memory leaks), the integrated component of ARIMA provides a
systematic de-trending mechanism that SMA lacks. (3) ARIMA(1,1,1) can be extended
to ARIMA(p,d,q) by adaptive order selection (AIC/BIC), allowing the system to be
tuned to the autocorrelation structure of each specific signature.
    Importantly, these reasons are conceptual and engineering-oriented, not empiri-
cal: the current benchmark does not provide evidence that ARIMA’s trend-modelling
advantage manifests in measurably better detection on Mozilla telemetry. Readers
who prioritise measured accuracy over interpretability or extensibility should consider
SMA as the deployment default.

8.3 When SMA Is the Better Choice
SMA retains the highest recall (0.657), detection rate (96%), and alert coverage in
the replay experiment (93%) among all evaluated methods. Deployment teams on
resource-constrained systems or requiring maximum recall and coverage should use

8.4 Precision Ceiling
Precision is low (< 0.12) for all methods. This is inherent to the dataset: Treeherder
regression alerts are based on a different detection algorithm (Bayesian change-point
detection), and many short performance excursions that DAET detects are real per-
formance anomalies that simply did not persist long enough for Mozilla’s system to
raise a formal regression ticket. The metric captures how many of our detection win-
dows coincide with official alerts, not whether the detected anomalies are genuine.
We therefore distinguish two precision concepts: “Treeherder agreement precision”



                                          27
(what Table 3 reports) and “true anomaly precision” (the fraction of detections corre-
sponding to genuine performance anomalies). The true anomaly precision is unknown
without manual inspection. A stronger validation would require (i) manual inspection
of a random sample of alleged false positives to classify them as genuine anomalies
vs. noise, or (ii) cross-referencing with a second alert source (e.g., Bugzilla regres-
sion tickets) to identify detections that correspond to real events not yet labelled by
Treeherder. We acknowledge this as a limitation and note it as directions for future
work.

8.5 Replay Signature Count Limitation
The replay experiment (Section 7) reports 136 alerted signatures, whereas the main
70%/30% evaluation reports 150. This 14-signature discrepancy arises because the
replay and main-evaluation pipelines are separate scripts that resolve the train–test
boundary from different intermediate CSV files; minor differences in which rows are
present cause a different Ttrain cutoff for 14 signatures, shifting their alerts out of
the test window. This is a data-provenance artefact of the multi-script 70%/30%
pipeline and does not affect the comparative conclusions across methods, since all four
detectors are affected identically. The three-way evaluation (Section 5.6) avoids this
inconsistency entirely by using a single unified pipeline (run full evaluation.py)
that applies the 60/20/20 split consistently across all methods and both datasets; it
reports 136 alerted signatures on Firefox-Android and 395 on mozilla-beta without
ambiguity. For future work, the replay experiment should be re-implemented within
the unified three-way pipeline to eliminate this source of inconsistency.

8.6 Scientific Conclusions vs. Deployment Recommendations
It is important to distinguish what the evidence supports scientifically from what it
supports as a deployment recommendation.
     What the evidence shows: In the main 70%/30% evaluation, SMA(w=20)
achieves the highest recall (0.657), detection rate (96.0%), and alert coverage in
the replay experiment; ARIMA and SMA are statistically indistinguishable in F1
(p = 0.547) and precision (p = 0.652). The three-way evaluation (60%/20%/20%)
on two datasets with a compact Transformer (five seeds, identical gating) provides
the fair learned-predictor comparison: F1 = 0.105 (±0.002) on Firefox-Android and
F1 = 0.079 (±0.001) on mozilla-beta, confirming that a learned forecasting back-
bone achieves classical-method-level detection quality when evaluated under equal
conditions.
     What the evidence does not show: The evidence does not show that any
single method is best for all workloads or all deployment contexts. The benchmark
is limited to one application (Firefox-Android), one telemetry platform (Treeherder),
and one operating period. Generalisation to other systems, seasonality regimes, or
alert-generation protocols requires further evaluation.
     Deployment recommendation: For deployments on resource-constrained sys-
tems, or where recall and alert coverage are the primary objectives, SMA(w=20) is the
recommended default: it is fast (30 s), deterministic, and achieves the highest detection


                                           28
rate (96%) and recall (0.657). For a learned predictor, the compact Transformer under
the 60%/20%/20% protocol (Section 5.6) is the recommended choice, with F1 = 0.105
on Firefox-Android and 0.079 on mozilla-beta. ARIMA(1,1,1) is appropriate when
explicit trend modelling, interpretable residuals, or extensibility to higher-order vari-
ants are required; its 27× runtime overhead (Table 4) should be factored into that
decision.

9 Threats to Validity
Internal validity. The four case studies (Section 4) are illustrative demonstrations
using known bug reports. While the framework’s candidate list was generated inde-
pendently of the bug report, the evaluation is retrospective and may not generalise
to bugs of a different character. The large-scale evaluation (Section 5) is prospective
in the sense that the detectors are trained on the historical period and evaluated on
a held-out chronological test set, but the main evaluation uses a two-way (70%/30%)
split. This limitation has been addressed in Section 5.6: a fully separate three-way
chronological split experiment (60%/20%/20%) was conducted in which SMA w and
EWMA α are independently selected on the held-out validation set and the test
set is evaluated only once. The three-way results confirm that the qualitative rank-
ing (SMA ≈ EWMA > ARIMA > LAST) is preserved under proper hold-out tuning
(Tables 7–8), and that the main conclusions are not an artefact of parameter selection
bias. The compact Transformer hyperparameters (window size, dmodel ) were tuned
on the held-out validation set; the test set was evaluated once. Results are reported
in Section 5.6 and Tables 7–8: on Firefox-Android the compact Transformer achieves
F1 = 0.105 (±0.002) and detection rate 74.4%; on mozilla-beta F1 = 0.079 (±0.001),
slightly below tuned SMA and EWMA.
    Construct validity. A critical limitation is that this paper evaluates agreement
with Treeherder alerts, not improvement in actual performance-debugging effec-
tiveness. Precision, recall, and F1 are computed relative to Treeherder’s Bayesian
change-point alerts, which are themselves imperfect: they may miss transient regres-
sions, include false positives from infrastructure changes, and are generated by a
different detection algorithm with different sensitivity settings. Consequently, the
paper does not show that DAET improves engineer productivity or reduces time-
to-root-cause—it shows that DAET’s binary output is correlated with an existing
automated alerting system. Whether higher Treeherder-agreement precision corre-
sponds to genuine improvement in debugging utility would require a controlled user
study or a longitudinal analysis linking DAET-triggered traces to bug-fix time. The
tolerance window τ = 5 samples introduces further matching leniency. All four sta-
tistical detectors use the same evaluation protocol, so the comparative findings are
unaffected by this construct choice, but all absolute precision and recall values should
be interpreted as “Treeherder-agreement metrics” rather than true anomaly precision
(see the Precision Ceiling discussion in Section 8). The false-positive counts have not
been manually adjudicated: a detection event classified as a false positive by Tree-
herder alignment may correspond to a genuine but unlabelled performance excursion.
A stricter validation would require manual inspection of a random sample of alleged



                                           29
false positives, or cross-referencing with a second alert source (e.g., Bugzilla regression
tickets).
    External validity. The evaluation uses two Mozilla Treeherder repositories
(Firefox-Android and mozilla-beta); both are from the same organisation and CI
platform, which limits generalisability to other applications, tracing granularities, or
monitoring infrastructures. The consistent qualitative ranking across both benchmarks
provides preliminary evidence that results extend beyond a single dataset, but vali-
dation on external CI systems remains future work. The replay experiment simulates
storage savings analytically; actual deployment savings depend on storage backend,
compression, and I/O scheduling.
    Conclusion validity. Statistical tests are Wilcoxon signed-rank on 214 paired
observations. The n = 214 sample size provides adequate power for detecting medium-
to-large effect sizes, but some genuinely small differences between methods may be
underpowered. With six pairwise comparisons among the four statistical detectors, a
Bonferroni-corrected threshold is α/6 = 0.008. All p < 0.001 cells satisfy this stricter
bound; borderline cells (p ∈ (0.001, 0.05] without asterisk) should be interpreted with
the family-wise correction in mind. The compact Transformer evaluated in Section 5.6
provides multi-seed confirmation: five independent seeds yield F1 std ≤ 0.002, con-
firming that training variability is small under the 60%/20%/20% protocol and does
not alter the qualitative conclusions.

10 Conclusion
This paper extended the Dynamic Adaptive Execution Tracing (DAET) framework
originally introduced in [1] with five new contributions: a large-scale comparative
evaluation of four classical change-detection engines, a compact Transformer encoder
under fair multi-seed protocol, a formal statistical analysis, a trace-on replay exper-
iment, and a three-way chronological split experiment. The journal contribution is
positioned as an empirical comparative extension : the core DAET framework archi-
tecture was established in the conference paper; this work provides the large-scale
prospective validation and comparative detector study that was explicitly deferred as
future work.
    The framework’s three-phase architecture—function selection via family-group
analysis and CoV ranking; change detection via a pluggable change-detection engine
with a dynamic anomaly-score threshold; and trace-configuration gating—was vali-
dated qualitatively through four Firefox Bugzilla case studies and quantitatively on 214
Mozilla Firefox-Android Treeherder telemetry signatures across 856 detector–signature
evaluation pairs.
    The large-scale evaluation showed that among the four statistical methods,
SMA(w=20) achieves the highest recall (0.657), F1 (0.186), and detection rate (96.0%),
making it the strongest statistical baseline. ARIMA(1,1,1) achieves F1 = 0.174
(95% CI: [0.154, 0.195]) and detection rate 88.67%, and is statistically indistinguishable
from SMA in F1 (p = 0.547) and precision (p = 0.652); both significantly outper-
form LAST and EWMA. A three-way chronological split experiment (60%/20%/20%)
across Firefox-Android and mozilla-beta with independent per-dataset validation-set



                                            30
tuning showed that properly tuned EWMA(α = 0.05) matches SMA in F1 on both
datasets (Firefox-Android: EWMA 0.103 vs. SMA 0.104; mozilla-beta: EWMA 0.083
vs. SMA 0.085). The qualitative ranking (SMA ≈ EWMA ≥ ARIMA > LAST) is con-
sistent across both datasets, supporting the generalisability of these findings beyond
a single benchmark. ARIMA(1,1,1) is an appropriate alternative when explicit trend
modelling, interpretable residuals, or extensibility to higher-order ARIMA variants
are operationally required; at 27× the runtime cost and 16× the memory footprint of
SMA (Table 4), it should not be used as the default engine on the basis of measured
accuracy alone. The replay experiment confirmed that all four statistical detectors
reduce trace storage by 89–91%, with SMA achieving the highest mean per-signature
alert recall (93.0%) and EWMA the highest storage reduction (91.0%). The ablation
study confirmed that the default parameters (β = 0.30, θ = 0.30, ARIMA(1,1,1),
τ = 5) are an operationally justified high-recall configuration, not globally optimal set-
tings: β = 0.20 yields ARIMA F1 = 0.266 (vs. the default 0.144), and θ ≥ 0.40 yields
F1 = 0.27 at the cost of lower recall. The comparative conclusions across methods are
stable across the full parameter range tested.
     The compact Transformer (five seeds, 60%/20%/20% protocol, dataset-specific HP
tuning) achieves F1 = 0.105 (±0.002) and detection rate 74.4% on Firefox-Android—
competitive with tuned SMA (F1 = 0.104)—and F1 = 0.079 (±0.001) on mozilla-
beta. Across both datasets the Transformer’s storage reduction (67–71%) is lower than
classical methods (88–90%), reflecting more frequent predictor activation; threshold
calibration is recommended for storage-constrained deployments.
     Future work. Open directions include: (i) validating the low-precision findings
against a manually annotated subset of alleged false positives and against Bugzilla
regression reports to estimate true anomaly precision; (ii) extending the frame-
work to seasonally non-stationary workloads using SARIMA or STL decomposition;
(iii) integrating anomaly score signals directly into an observability platform (e.g.,
Prometheus/Grafana) for real-time deployment; (iv) evaluating the framework on
additional CI/CD pipelines beyond Mozilla Firefox; and (v) extending the dual-dataset
evaluation to additional Mozilla CI projects and to external production systems
beyond Firefox to assess cross-domain generalisability of the qualitative ranking.
